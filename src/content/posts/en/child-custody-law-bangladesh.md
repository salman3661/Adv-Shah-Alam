  - h2: "What Is Hizanat (Maternal Custody)?"
    content: "<p>Under Islamic law as applied in Bangladesh, <strong>hizanat</strong> refers to the primary right of the mother to have physical custody of young children. The age limits under classical Islamic jurisprudence (as applied traditionally) are:</p>\n<ul>\n<li><strong>Sons:</strong> Till approximately 7 years of age</li>\n<li><strong>Daughters:</strong> Till puberty</li>\n</ul>\n<p>However, Bangladesh courts do not apply these age rules rigidly. If the mother has remarried, is morally unfit, or cannot adequately care for the child, the court may reduce or override hizanat. The father retains <strong>guardianship</strong> throughout — he is legally responsible for the child's upbringing, education, and maintenance even if the mother has physical custody.</p>"
  - h2: "Father's Rights and Responsibilities"
    content: "<p>Under both Islamic law and the Guardians and Wards Act, the father is the natural guardian of the child in Bangladesh. This means:</p>\n<ul>\n<li>The father has the right and duty to make decisions about the child's education, religion, and major life events.</li>\n<li>Financial maintenance of the child is exclusively the father's obligation.</li>\n<li>Once boys reach approximately 7 years and girls reach puberty, custody may move to the father if the mother's hizanat period ends — but courts still apply the welfare test.</li>\n</ul>\n<p>A father who is denied access to his children can apply for visitation rights or custody in the Family Court regardless of the mother's hizanat rights.</p>"
  - h2: "How Family Courts Decide Custody"
    content: "<p>The Family Court weighs all relevant facts including:</p>\n<ul>\n<li>Age and gender of the child</li>\n<li>Physical and mental health of both parents</li>\n<li>Financial ability of each parent to provide for the child</li>\n<li>The child's own wishes (if old enough to express them clearly)</li>\n<li>Which parent has been the primary caregiver historically</li>\n<li>Any history of domestic violence, substance abuse, or neglect</li>\n<li>Educational stability and continuity for the child</li>\n</ul>\n<p>Courts in Bangladesh increasingly prioritise joint parental involvement rather than complete exclusion of one parent.</p>"
  - h2: "The Best Interest of the Child Principle"
    content: "<p>The <strong>welfare or best interest of the child</strong> is the paramount consideration in all custody decisions. Bangladesh courts have consistently held that no religious rule or parental preference can override what is truly best for the child's physical, emotional, and moral development.</p>\n<p>This principle was firmly established in landmark High Court Division decisions that override rigid application of age-old hizanat rules when circumstances demand it. Courts have granted mothers custody of older boys and fathers custody of young daughters when the facts warranted it.</p>"
