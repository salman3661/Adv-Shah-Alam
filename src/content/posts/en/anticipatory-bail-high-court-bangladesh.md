  - h2: "Legal Basis for High Court Anticipatory Bail"
    content: "<p>The High Court Division's power to grant anticipatory bail flows from multiple sources:</p>\n<ul>\n<li><strong>Article 102 of the Constitution:</strong> The High Court Division's inherent writ jurisdiction allows it to protect persons from unlawful or oppressive arrest.</li>\n<li><strong>Section 498 of the CrPC:</strong> Expressly empowers \"any court\" — including the High Court Division — to grant bail.</li>\n<li><strong>Section 561A CrPC:</strong> The High Court Division's inherent powers to make orders necessary to prevent abuse of the process of court or to secure the ends of justice.</li>\n</ul>\n<p>Bangladesh Supreme Court case law has consistently affirmed the High Court Division's power to grant anticipatory bail in appropriate cases, particularly where lower court orders are inadequate or where the accusation is prima facie motivated by malice.</p>"
  - h2: "Step-by-Step Procedure at the High Court Division"
    content: "<p>The procedure for obtaining anticipatory bail from the High Court Division involves these steps:</p>\n<ol>\n<li><strong>Retain a High Court-enrolled lawyer</strong> — only lawyers enrolled to appear before the Supreme Court's High Court Division can file and argue this application.</li>\n<li><strong>Prepare the bail petition</strong> — a detailed application setting out: the FIR details (case number, police station, sections alleged), factual background, grounds for believing the accusation is false or motivated, why arrest would cause irreparable harm, and why the applicant is not a flight risk.</li>\n<li><strong>File in the Criminal Miscellaneous jurisdiction</strong> — the application is filed as a Criminal Miscellaneous Petition in the High Court Registry. A court fee is payable.</li>\n<li><strong>Request interim anticipatory bail</strong> — simultaneously with filing, request urgent interim bail (stay of arrest) until the full hearing. Many courts grant this on the first day of filing if the application is strong.</li>\n<li><strong>First hearing / filing date</strong> — the case is placed before a bench of the High Court Division. The State (prosecution) is given notice.</li>\n<li><strong>Full hearing</strong> — both sides argue. Your lawyer presents the legal grounds; the State advocates for denial.</li>\n<li><strong>Order</strong> — the court grants or refuses. Conditions are typically attached if bail is granted.</li>\n</ol>"
  - h2: "Documents Required for High Court Bail Application"
    content: "<p>A well-supported application needs:</p>\n<ul>\n<li>Certified copy of the FIR (obtained from the relevant police station through a lawyer)</li>\n<li>NID and passport of the applicant</li>\n<li>Sworn affidavit by the applicant detailing all material facts</li>\n<li>Sessions Court order (if bail was rejected there — this becomes part of the High Court record)</li>\n<li>Supporting evidence: messages, CCTV footage, alibi documents, bank records, character certificates</li>\n<li>Vakalatnama (power of attorney for the lawyer)</li>\n<li>Any Court fee receipts</li>\n</ul>\n<p>The affidavit is crucial — it must be truthful, complete, and clear. The court will not look favourably on an application where material facts are suppressed.</p>"
  - h2: "What the Court Examines"
    content: "<p>The High Court Division assesses anticipatory bail applications by weighing:</p>\n<ul>\n<li><strong>Nature and gravity of the accusation</strong> — is this a minor offence or a serious crime involving death, terrorism, or rape?</li>\n<li><strong>The applicant's criminal history</strong> — a first-time accused with no prior record fares better.</li>\n<li><strong>Prima facie merits of the FIR</strong> — does the accusation appear credible or does it seem to be an exaggerated or false complaint?</li>\n<li><strong>Flight risk</strong> — ties to the community, family, business, employment; possession of a passport and travel history.</li>\n<li><strong>Risk of evidence tampering</strong> — would releasing the applicant allow them to influence witnesses?</li>\n<li><strong>Balance of convenience</strong> — would the harm to an innocent person from pre-trial detention outweigh the State's interest in custody?</li>\n</ul>\n<p>The strongest applications are those where the FIR appears motivated, no prior criminal record exists, the applicant has strong community ties, and credible exculpatory evidence is placed on record.</p>"
