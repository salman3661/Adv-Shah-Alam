  - h2: "Marriage Law in Bangladesh"
    content: "<p>Key rules around marriage in Bangladesh:</p>\n<ul>\n<li><strong>Muslim Marriage (Nikah):</strong> Must be registered with a licensed Nikah Registrar (Kazi). The Kabinnama (marriage certificate) is the formal record. Without registration, the marriage is not void but is much harder to prove legally.</li>\n<li><strong>Minimum age:</strong> 18 for women, 21 for men under the Child Marriage Restraint Act 2017. Marriage below this age is prohibited (though legally contracted marriages below these ages are not automatically void).</li>\n<li><strong>Mahr (Dower):</strong> A mandatory gift from husband to wife specified in the Kabinnama. It is the wife's legal right and can be demanded at any time.</li>\n<li><strong>Second marriage:</strong> Under the MFLO 1961, a husband must obtain written permission from the existing wife AND the Arbitration Council before taking a second wife.</li>\n<li><strong>Court Marriage:</strong> Non-Muslim or inter-faith couples can register under the Special Marriage Act.</li>\n</ul>"
  - h2: "Divorce Law in Bangladesh"
    content: "<p>Bangladesh law recognises multiple forms of divorce for Muslims:</p>\n<ul>\n<li><strong>Talaq:</strong> Husband-initiated. Requires written notice to Union Parishad Chairman + 90-day waiting period.</li>\n<li><strong>Khula:</strong> Wife-initiated (with husband's agreement). Wife typically returns mahr.</li>\n<li><strong>Mubarat:</strong> Mutual consent divorce.</li>\n<li><strong>Judicial Divorce:</strong> Wife files in Family Court when husband refuses, on specific legal grounds (cruelty, non-maintenance, imprisonment etc.).</li>\n</ul>\n<p>All divorce notifications must go through the Union Parishad Chairman. Failure to notify is a criminal offence under Section 6 of the MFLO. The mandatory 90-day reconciliation period applies to all extrajudicial divorce types. Learn more: <a href=\"/divorce-process-bangladesh-2026\" style=\"color:var(--accent)\">Complete divorce process guide (2026)</a>.</p>"
  - h2: "Child Custody Under Bangladesh Law"
    content: "<p>Child custody (Hizanat) rules in Bangladesh:</p>\n<ul>\n<li><strong>Mother's right:</strong> Physical custody of sons until age 7; daughters until puberty (approximately 15).</li>\n<li><strong>Father's right:</strong> Legal guardianship. Physical custody after Hizanat ages, and if the mother becomes unfit.</li>\n<li><strong>Court standard:</strong> The child's best interests override rigid age rules. Courts consider welfare, safety, stability, and the child's own wishes for older children.</li>\n<li><strong>Procedure:</strong> Apply under the Guardian and Wards Act 1890 at the Family Court.</li>\n</ul>\n<p>Custody is one of the most contested areas of family law. Learn more: <a href=\"/child-custody-law-bangladesh-2026\" style=\"color:var(--accent)\">Child custody law guide (2026)</a>.</p>"
  - h2: "Maintenance Rights in Bangladesh"
    content: "<p>Maintenance (nafaqah) is a critical right under Bangladesh family law:</p>\n<ul>\n<li><strong>Wife's right:</strong> A husband must maintain his wife during the marriage. After divorce, he must pay iddat maintenance (3 months) and the outstanding mahr.</li>\n<li><strong>Child maintenance:</strong> A father must maintain all children regardless of custody arrangements. This obligation continues until daughters marry and sons become self-sufficient.</li>\n<li><strong>How to claim:</strong> Apply to the Family Court for a maintenance order. The court can also order interim maintenance while the case is pending.</li>\n<li><strong>Enforcement:</strong> Non-payment of court-ordered maintenance can result in civil proceedings or even imprisonment in some circumstances.</li>\n</ul>"
  - h2: "Dower (Mahr) – What Is It and How to Claim It?"
    content: "<p>Mahr is a mandatory gift specified in the Kabinnama that the husband owes to the wife. It has two components:</p>\n<ul>\n<li><strong>Prompt dower (mu'ajjal):</strong> Payable immediately on demand by the wife.</li>\n<li><strong>Deferred dower (mu'wajjal):</strong> Payable on death of the husband or on divorce.</li>\n</ul>\n<p>On divorce, the <em>entire outstanding mahr</em> becomes immediately payable. This is a legal right — not a negotiation point. A wife can sue in Family Court to recover unpaid mahr. Many women in Bangladesh are unaware of this right and lose it by delay or by accepting inadequate verbal settlements.</p>"
  - h2: "Inheritance Law in Bangladesh"
    content: "<p>Inheritance for Muslims in Bangladesh follows Islamic law (Shariat):</p>\n<ul>\n<li>Daughters receive half the share of sons as heirs</li>\n<li>Wives receive 1/8 share if there are children; 1/4 if childless</li>\n<li>Mothers receive 1/6 in most standard cases</li>\n<li>Non-Muslims cannot inherit from Muslims (and vice versa in most interpretations)</li>\n</ul>\n<p>Property disputes involving inheritance are very common in Bangladesh and are handled by the Civil Court system. A family lawyer specialising in property and inheritance can help navigate these matters, particularly when there are multiple heirs or disputed wills.</p>"

  - h2: "The Family Court System – How It Works"
    content: "<p>Bangladesh's Family Court system was established by the Family Courts Ordinance 1985. Key facts:</p>\n<ul>\n<li>Family Courts have exclusive jurisdiction over: divorce, maintenance, dower, restitution of conjugal rights, and child custody.</li>\n<li>There is one Family Court (presided over by a Judge of the District Court) per district.</li>\n<li>Appeals from Family Court decisions go to the District Court, then to the High Court Division.</li>\n<li>Family Court proceedings can be conducted without a lawyer, but legal representation is strongly recommended given the complexity of the issues.</li>\n</ul>"
