  - h2: "Key Rights of Workers Under the Labour Act"
    content: "<p>The Bangladesh Labour Act 2006 guarantees workers the following rights:</p>\n<ul>\n<li><strong>Minimum wage:</strong> Wages must not fall below the minimum wage set by the National Minimum Wage Board for each sector.</li>\n<li><strong>Payment on time:</strong> Wages must be paid by the 7th working day of the following month.</li>\n<li><strong>Maximum working hours:</strong> Workers cannot generally be required to work more than <strong>8 hours per day</strong> or <strong>48 hours per week</strong>. Overtime (at 200% of the basic wage rate) must be paid for additional hours.</li>\n<li><strong>Annual leave:</strong> Workers are entitled to one day of paid leave for every 18 days worked.</li>\n<li><strong>Sick leave:</strong> 14 days of sick leave with full wages per year.</li>\n<li><strong>Festival leave:</strong> 11 days per year for gazetted public holidays.</li>\n<li><strong>Casual leave:</strong> 10 days per year.</li>\n</ul>\n<p>Any clause in an employment contract that provides <em>less</em> than these statutory minimums is unenforceable — the law sets a floor, not a ceiling.</p>"
  - h2: "Employment Contracts and Appointment Letters"
    content: "<p>Under the BLA 2006, every employer is required to issue an <strong>appointment letter</strong> at the time of employment, specifying:</p>\n<ul>\n<li>Nature of work and designation</li>\n<li>Basic wage and allowances</li>\n<li>Working hours and leave entitlements</li>\n<li>Period of probation (if applicable)</li>\n<li>Notice period for termination</li>\n</ul>\n<p>Failure to issue an appointment letter is itself a violation of the Labour Act. Employees who did not receive one can raise this in a labour court proceeding. For workers employed for more than <strong>3 months</strong> (non-probationary), more stringent rules apply to termination.</p>"
  - h2: "Termination of Employment: Rules and Notice"
    content: "<p>The BLA 2006 distinguishes between different types of severance:</p>\n<ul>\n<li><strong>Notice of termination:</strong> A permanent worker must be given <strong>120 days' notice</strong> (or pay in lieu) if terminated without cause. For monthly-rated workers, 30 days' notice applies during probation.</li>\n<li><strong>Dismissal for misconduct:</strong> Requires a formal inquiry — the worker must be given a charge sheet, an opportunity to respond, and a departmental inquiry before any dismissal order is passed.</li>\n<li><strong>Retrenchment (reduction in workforce):</strong> Requires one month's notice and compensation at the rate of 30 days' wages for each completed year of service.</li>\n<li><strong>Lay-off:</strong> Workers cannot be indefinitely laid off without compensation. Rules for lay-off compensation are specified under the Act.</li>\n</ul>\n<p>Wrongful termination (dismissal without following due procedure) entitles the worker to reinstatement or compensation through the Labour Court. If you have been dismissed without proper process, contact a <a href=\"/criminal-lawyer-uttara\" style=\"color:var(--accent)\">labour law expert in Dhaka</a> immediately.</p>"
  - h2: "Gratuity, Provident Fund, and Leave Encashment"
    content: "<p>Workers employed for <strong>5 or more continuous years</strong> are entitled to <strong>gratuity</strong> upon retirement, resignation, termination, or death:</p>\n<ul>\n<li>Gratuity rate: <strong>30 days' basic wage</strong> for each completed year of service (as per 2018 amendment)</li>\n<li>If the worker has a provident fund, the employer's contribution plus accumulated interest is also payable</li>\n</ul>\n<p>Workers dismissed for gross misconduct may forfeit gratuity. However, the Labour Court can review whether the grounds for forfeiture were justified.</p>\n<p>Workers who have accumulated earned leave that they did not take are entitled to <strong>leave encashment</strong> — cash payment for unused leave — calculated at the daily wage rate. Failure to pay gratuity or leave encashment within the prescribed period is an offence under the Act.</p>"
  - h2: "Industrial Disputes and the Labour Court"
    content: "<p>Labour disputes in Bangladesh are resolved through a tiered system:</p>\n<ol>\n<li><strong>Bipartite negotiation:</strong> Employer and worker (or their representatives) attempt to resolve the dispute internally.</li>\n<li><strong>Conciliation by the Department of Labour:</strong> If negotiation fails, either party can apply to the Chief Inspector of Factories and Establishments or the Labour Department for conciliation.</li>\n<li><strong>Labour Court:</strong> If conciliation fails, the dispute is referred to the Labour Court. Bangladesh has District Labour Courts in major cities including Dhaka. The court can order reinstatement, back wages, compensation, or a combination.</li>\n<li><strong>Labour Appellate Tribunal:</strong> Appeals from the Labour Court go to the Labour Appellate Tribunal.</li>\n</ol>\n<p>There is a specific limitation period for labour disputes — delayed claims can be rejected. Act promptly and consult a lawyer as soon as a dispute arises.</p>"
  - h2: "Trade Unions in Bangladesh"
    content: "<p>Workers in Bangladesh have the right to form and join trade unions under the BLA 2006. Key rules:</p>\n<ul>\n<li>A trade union requires a minimum of <strong>30% of total workers</strong> in a workplace or sector to register</li>\n<li>Unions must be registered with the Department of Labour</li>\n<li>Collective bargaining agents (CBA) are elected to negotiate with management</li>\n<li>Unfair labour practices — such as victimising union leaders, threatening workers who join unions, or discriminating in employment — are prohibited</li>\n</ul>\n<p>Anti-union discrimination is a serious violation and can be brought before the Labour Court. Workers who suffer adverse action because of trade union membership can seek reinstatement and compensation.</p>"
