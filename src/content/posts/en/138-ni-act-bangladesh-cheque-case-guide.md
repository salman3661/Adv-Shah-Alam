  - h2: "What Is Section 138 of the Negotiable Instruments Act?"
    content: "<p>Section 138 of the <strong>Negotiable Instruments Act 1881 (NI Act)</strong> — as it applies in Bangladesh — makes it a criminal offence to issue a cheque that is dishonoured by the bank due to:</p>\n<ul>\n<li>Insufficient funds in the account</li>\n<li>The amount exceeding the arrangement</li>\n<li>Account closed or frozen</li>\n</ul>\n<p>The law exists to protect payment systems and commercial trust. A cheque is a legal promise to pay — if that promise is broken, the law allows the recipient to pursue criminal action. Section 138 NI Act cases are one of the most common commercial disputes handled by courts in Bangladesh today.</p>"
  - h2: "When Is Cheque Dishonour a Crime in Bangladesh?"
    content: "<p>Not every bounced cheque creates criminal liability. The following conditions must ALL be met:</p>\n<ol>\n<li>The cheque was issued for the <strong>discharge of a legally enforceable debt or liability</strong> — not as a gift or under duress.</li>\n<li>The cheque was presented to the bank <strong>within 6 months</strong> of the date on the cheque (or within the cheque's validity period, whichever is earlier).</li>\n<li>The bank <strong>returned the cheque unpaid</strong> (with a memo stating reason: insufficient funds, account closed etc.).</li>\n<li>The payee sent a <strong>legal notice</strong> to the drawer (cheque issuer) within <strong>30 days</strong> of receiving the bank's dishonour memo.</li>\n<li>The drawer <strong>failed to make payment</strong> within <strong>30 days</strong> of receiving the legal notice.</li>\n</ol>\n<p>Only after all five conditions are met can a criminal complaint be filed.</p>"
  - h2: "Step 1: Send the Legal Notice (Mandatory)"
    content: "<p>The legal notice is the most critical procedural step — without it, your case will be dismissed. Here is what must happen:</p>\n<ol>\n<li>After receiving the bank's dishonour memo, send a <strong>written legal notice</strong> to the cheque drawer within <strong>30 days</strong>. This deadline is absolute.</li>\n<li>The notice must:</li>\n</ol>\n<ul>\n<li>Be addressed to the drawer at their known address</li>\n<li>State the cheque details: cheque number, date, amount, drawee bank</li>\n<li>State that the cheque was dishonoured and the reason</li>\n<li>Demand payment of the full amount within 30 days of the notice</li>\n<li>Warn that criminal action will be taken if payment is not received</li>\n</ul>\n<ol start=\"3\">\n<li>Send the notice by <strong>registered post</strong> AND retain the postal receipt and delivery acknowledgment.</li>\n</ol>\n<p>A lawyer-drafted notice is significantly more effective than a self-written one. Contact a <a href=\"/criminal-lawyer-uttara\" style=\"color:var(--accent)\">criminal lawyer in Dhaka</a> immediately upon receiving a dishonoured cheque.</p>"
  - h2: "Step 2: File the Case in Court"
    content: "<p>If the drawer does not pay within 30 days of receiving the legal notice:</p>\n<ol>\n<li>Your lawyer files a <strong>complaint petition</strong> under Section 138 NI Act before the appropriate court.</li>\n<li>The complaint must be filed within <strong>30 days</strong> of the expiry of the 30-day payment period in the notice. This is a <strong>strict limitation period</strong>.</li>\n<li>The court determines if the prima facie case is made out and issues a summons to the accused.</li>\n<li>The accused appears before the court and enters a plea.</li>\n<li>Trial proceedings begin — the complainant presents evidence; the accused may cross-examine and present their defence.</li>\n<li>Court delivers verdict: conviction or acquittal.</li>\n</ol>"
  - h2: "Which Court Has Jurisdiction?"
    content: "<p>Section 138 NI Act cases in Bangladesh are tried as criminal cases. Jurisdiction:</p>\n<ul>\n<li><strong>Chief Metropolitan Magistrate (CMM) Court</strong> or <strong>Chief Judicial Magistrate (CJM) Court</strong> — depending on whether the case is in a Metropolitan area (Dhaka, Chittagong, Rajshahi) or district.</li>\n<li>The case should be filed in the court where the cheque was dishonoured — i.e., where the bank branch holding the payee's account is located.</li>\n</ul>\n<p>This jurisdictional point is frequently contested. Your lawyer must identify the correct court to avoid dismissal on jurisdictional grounds.</p>"
  - h2: "Evidence Required to Win a 138 NI Act Case"
    content: "<p>To successfully prosecute a Section 138 case, you need:</p>\n<ul>\n<li>Original dishonoured cheque</li>\n<li>Bank's dishonour memo / return memo</li>\n<li>Copy of legal notice sent to the drawer</li>\n<li>Proof of delivery of the notice (postal acknowledgment / courier receipt)</li>\n<li>Proof that 30 days passed without payment from the drawer</li>\n<li>Proof that the cheque was issued for a legally enforceable debt (contract, invoice, statement of account)</li>\n</ul>\n<p>The complainant bears the initial burden of proof. Once the complainant establishes the prima facie case, the burden shifts to the accused to prove a valid defence.</p>"
  - h2: "Punishment Under Section 138"
    content: "<p>On conviction under Section 138 NI Act in Bangladesh, the accused can be sentenced to:</p>\n<ul>\n<li><strong>Imprisonment</strong> up to <strong>1 year</strong>, and/or</li>\n<li><strong>Fine</strong> up to twice the amount of the dishonoured cheque</li>\n</ul>\n<p>Additionally, courts commonly order the payment of the cheque amount to the complainant as part of the sentence or through civil proceedings run concurrently. The combination of criminal and financial consequences makes Section 138 a powerful legal tool.</p>"
  - h2: "The Defences Available to the Accused"
    content: "<p>If you have been accused under Section 138, these defences may be available:</p>\n<ul>\n<li><strong>The cheque was not issued for a debt/liability</strong> — e.g., it was a security cheque given without any underlying debt.</li>\n<li><strong>The debt was not legally enforceable</strong> — e.g., time-barred debt.</li>\n<li><strong>The legal notice was not received</strong> — but be aware: courts presume service of notice if it was properly sent by registered post.</li>\n<li><strong>The notice was not sent within 30 days</strong> of the dishonour — a valid procedural defence.</li>\n<li><strong>The complaint was filed after the limitation period</strong>.</li>\n<li><strong>The cheque was stolen or forged</strong> — requires strong evidence.</li>\n<li><strong>Payment was made after receiving the notice</strong> — though this does not fully extinguish the offence, it mitigates punishment.</li>\n</ul>"

  - question: "What is Section 138 NI Act in Bangladesh?"
    answer: "Section 138 of the Negotiable Instruments Act 1881 makes it a criminal offence in Bangladesh to issue a cheque that bounces due to insufficient funds or a closed account. Punishment includes up to 1 year imprisonment and/or a fine of twice the cheque amount."
  - question: "What is the time limit to file a cheque dishonour case in Bangladesh?"
    answer: "The legal notice must be sent within 30 days of the bank's dishonour memo. If the drawer doesn't pay within 30 days of the notice, the court complaint must be filed within the next 30 days. Missing any deadline can invalidate the case."
  - question: "Is a legal notice mandatory before filing a Section 138 NI Act case?"
    answer: "Yes, absolutely mandatory. Sending a legal notice to the drawer within 30 days of dishonour is a required precondition. Failure to send the notice will result in the case being dismissed."
  - question: "What documents are needed to file a cheque dishonour case in Bangladesh?"
    answer: "Original dishonoured cheque, bank's dishonour memo, copy of legal notice, proof of notice delivery, evidence that 30 days passed without payment, and documentary proof supporting the underlying debt."
  - question: "Can cheque dishonour cases be settled out of court in Bangladesh?"
    answer: "Yes. After a case is filed, parties can settle — the accused pays the cheque amount plus agreed compensation and the complainant withdraws the case. Courts generally permit this. Settlement is a common outcome."
  - question: "What is a security cheque and is it covered by Section 138?"
    answer: "A security cheque (given as guarantee, not for immediate payment) is a contested area. Courts have varied rulings. Generally, if the security cheque represents a genuine underlying obligation that was enforceable, Section 138 can apply — but the specific facts matter greatly."
