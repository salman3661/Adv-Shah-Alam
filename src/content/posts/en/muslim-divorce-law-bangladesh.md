  - h2: "The Legal Framework: Muslim Divorce in Bangladesh"
    content: "<p>Muslim divorce in Bangladesh is governed primarily by:</p>\n<ul>\n<li><strong>Muslim Family Laws Ordinance 1961 (MFLO):</strong> The foundational statutory law governing Muslim divorce in Bangladesh. Sections 6 and 7 impose the notification and waiting period requirements.</li>\n<li><strong>Muslim Personal Law (Shariat) Application Act 1937:</strong> Applies Islamic personal law principles where the MFLO is silent.</li>\n<li><strong>Family Courts Ordinance 1985:</strong> Governs judicial divorce proceedings in the Family Court.</li>\n<li><strong>Dissolution of Muslim Marriages Act 1939:</strong> Provides specific grounds on which a Muslim wife can obtain a judicial decree of divorce.</li>\n</ul>\n<p>Bangladesh courts have consistently ruled that these laws must be strictly followed. Any procedural deviation — even if unintentional — can have significant legal consequences for the divorce's validity.</p>"
  - h2: "Types of Muslim Divorce Under Bangladesh Law"
    content: "<p>Under Muslim law as applied in Bangladesh, divorce takes these main forms:</p>\n<ul>\n<li><strong>Talaq:</strong> Husband's unilateral right to divorce. Most common form.</li>\n<li><strong>Khula:</strong> Wife-initiated divorce by agreement with the husband (usually by returning mahr).</li>\n<li><strong>Mubarat:</strong> Mutual divorce by agreement of both parties.</li>\n<li><strong>Faskh (Judicial Divorce):</strong> Court-pronounced dissolution on the wife's petition, on specific legal grounds under the Dissolution of Muslim Marriages Act 1939.</li>\n<li><strong>Delegated Divorce (Talaq-e-Tawfiz):</strong> A right of divorce delegated to the wife in the Kabinnama itself, allowing her to pronounce divorce under specified conditions.</li>\n</ul>"
  - h2: "Talaq – Rules, Process & Legal Requirements"
    content: "<p>In Bangladesh, talaq is not simply about the husband saying 'I divorce you.' The MFLO 1961 imposes mandatory requirements:</p>\n<ol>\n<li>The husband pronounces talaq — verbally or in writing.</li>\n<li>The husband must send a <strong>written notice</strong> to the Chairman of the local Union Parishad / Ward Commissioner within reasonable time after pronouncement <em>and simultaneously</em> serve a copy on the wife.</li>\n<li>The Chairman constitutes an Arbitration Council within 30 days.</li>\n<li>The Arbitration Council attempts reconciliation over the <strong>90-day waiting period</strong> from notice receipt.</li>\n<li>If reconciliation fails: talaq is effective on Day 90. The Chairman issues a Divorce Certificate.</li>\n</ol>\n<p><strong>Critical rule on Triple Talaq:</strong> Under Bangladesh law, even if the husband pronounces talaq three times in one sitting, it is treated as a SINGLE REVOCABLE talaq — not as three separate or instant divorces. The MFLO 1961 overrides the classical interpretation in this respect. The notification-and-waiting-period procedure must still be followed.</p>"
  - h2: "Khula – The Wife's Right to Divorce"
    content: "<p>Khula is the Islamic right of a wife to seek release from marriage, typically by offering to return her mahr (dower) or part of it to the husband in exchange for his agreement to divorce her.</p>\n<p>In Bangladesh, khula is a negotiated arrangement:</p>\n<ol>\n<li>The wife communicates her desire to end the marriage to her husband.</li>\n<li>The parties negotiate what consideration the wife returns.</li>\n<li>On agreement, the khula notice is sent to the UP Chairman.</li>\n<li>The same 90-day reconciliation procedure applies.</li>\n<li>Divorce certificate issued after 90 days if no reconciliation.</li>\n</ol>\n<p>If the husband refuses to agree to khula, the wife's legal remedy is a judicial divorce (faskh) under the Dissolution of Muslim Marriages Act 1939 through the Family Court. The wife does not need the husband's consent for a judicial divorce on the specified grounds.</p>"
  - h2: "Mubarat – Mutual Consent Divorce"
    content: "<p>Mubarat is a divorce initiated by mutual agreement of both husband and wife. Both parties jointly send the notice to the Union Parishad Chairman. Key features:</p>\n<ul>\n<li>Requires genuine mutual consent — not coercion</li>\n<li>The same 90-day rule applies, but since both agree, reconciliation is unlikely to succeed and the divorce proceeds to finality</li>\n<li>Can address financial and custody matters in the same written agreement</li>\n<li>Generally the fastest and least adversarial form of divorce</li>\n</ul>\n<p>It is strongly recommended to have a lawyer document the agreed financial terms (mahr, maintenance, property division) and child custody arrangements in writing before the 90 days expire.</p>"
  - h2: "Judicial Divorce Through Family Court"
    content: "<p>Under the <strong>Dissolution of Muslim Marriages Act 1939</strong>, a Muslim wife (and in some cases a husband) can obtain a Court divorce on the following grounds:</p>\n<ul>\n<li>whereabouts of husband unknown for 4+ years</li>\n<li>failure to maintain wife for 2+ years</li>\n<li>husband sentenced to 7+ years imprisonment</li>\n<li>failure of husband to perform marital obligations for 3+ years</li>\n<li>husband's impotence at time of marriage</li>\n<li>husband's insanity for 2+ years</li>\n<li>repudiation of marriage before consummation (where wife was minor)</li>\n<li>cruelty (physical or mental)</li>\n<li>any other ground recognised under Muslim law</li>\n</ul>\n<p>File the divorce petition (plaint) at the Family Court in your district. Timelines depend on complexity — from 6 months to several years for contested cases.</p>"
  - h2: "The 90-Day Rule – What It Means & Why It Cannot Be Skipped"
    content: "<p>Section 7 of the MFLO 1961 mandates a 90-day waiting period after the Union Parishad Chairman receives the talaq/khula/mubarat notice. During this period:</p>\n<ul>\n<li>The Arbitration Council attempts reconciliation</li>\n<li>The divorce is NOT yet effective — the parties are still legally married</li>\n<li>The husband cannot remarry during this period</li>\n<li>If the wife is pregnant, the divorce effect is postponed until the birth of the child</li>\n</ul>\n<p><strong>Why it cannot be skipped:</strong> Section 6 of the MFLO makes it a criminal offence (punishable by fine and/or imprisonment up to 1 year) to divorce without following the notice procedure. The divorce is also deemed legally ineffective until the notice requirements are properly completed. Courts have confirmed this repeatedly.</p>"
  - h2: "Rights of the Wife After Muslim Divorce"
    content: "<p>After a Muslim divorce in Bangladesh, the wife has these legal rights:</p>\n<ul>\n<li><strong>Full Mahr:</strong> The entire outstanding mahr becomes due and payable immediately on divorce. This is an absolute legal right, not a negotiating point.</li>\n<li><strong>Iddat Maintenance:</strong> The husband must maintain the wife for the iddat period (3 months or until delivery if pregnant) after divorce takes effect.</li>\n<li><strong>Return of Dowry:</strong> All gifts and jewelry given at marriage must be returned.</li>\n<li><strong>Child Maintenance:</strong> The father remains liable for child support regardless of custody.</li>\n<li><strong>Custody (Hizanat):</strong> Mother has primary custody of young children (sons to 7, daughters to puberty).</li>\n</ul>\n<p>These rights are enforceable through the Family Court. Do not accept verbal settlements — get everything documented in writing and reviewed by a lawyer.</p>"
  - h2: "Rights of the Husband After Muslim Divorce"
    content: "<p>After divorce, the husband retains:</p>\n<ul>\n<li>The right to remarry (after the divorce is effective on Day 90, and after his own iddat if applicable)</li>\n<li>Legal guardianship of the children (even while the mother has physical custody under Hizanat)</li>\n<li>Right to custody of children when they exceed the Hizanat ages (sons after 7, daughters after puberty)</li>\n<li>Right to challenge custody at the Family Court if the mother becomes unfit</li>\n</ul>\n<p>However, the husband must continue to pay child maintenance regardless of custody. Failure to do so is enforceable by court.</p>"
  - h2: "Common Myths About Muslim Divorce in Bangladesh"
    content: "<ul>\n<li><strong>Myth: \"Saying talaq three times instantly ends the marriage.\"</strong><br/>Reality: Under Bangladesh law (MFLO 1961), even triple talaq in one sitting is treated as a single revocable talaq requiring the full notification-and-waiting-period procedure.</li>\n<li><strong>Myth: \"Only the husband can give divorce.\"</strong><br/>Reality: A wife can seek khula (by agreement) or judicial divorce in Family Court on legal grounds, without the husband's consent.</li>\n<li><strong>Myth: \"Divorce by phone or WhatsApp is valid.\"</strong><br/>Reality: While the mode of pronouncement may be recognized, the written notice to the UP Chairman is always mandatory and cannot be replaced by a phone call or message.</li>\n<li><strong>Myth: \"Mahr can be negotiated down or waived after divorce.\"</strong><br/>Reality: A wife cannot be legally compelled to waive her mahr. Any waiver obtained under duress or without proper informed consent is potentially voidable.</li>\n<li><strong>Myth: \"Divorce means the father has no more responsibility for the children.\"</strong><br/>Reality: The father's obligation to maintain his children continues after divorce regardless of who has custody.</li>\n</ul>"
faqs:
  - question: "Is triple talaq valid in Bangladesh?"
    answer: "No. Under the MFLO 1961, even triple talaq in one sitting is treated as a single revocable talaq in Bangladesh. The standard notification and 90-day waiting period procedure must still be followed."
  - question: "What is the difference between talaq and khula in Muslim law in Bangladesh?"
    answer: "Talaq is the husband's unilateral right to divorce. Khula is the wife's right to seek divorce through agreement with the husband, typically by returning mahr. Both require notifying the UP Chairman and waiting 90 days."
  - question: "Can a wife divorce her husband without his consent under Muslim law in Bangladesh?"
    answer: "Yes, through judicial divorce (faskh) filed in Family Court under the Dissolution of Muslim Marriages Act 1939 on specific legal grounds such as cruelty, non-maintenance, or the husband's imprisonment."
  - question: "What is the MFLO 1961 and why is it important for divorce?"
    answer: "The Muslim Family Laws Ordinance 1961 is the primary statute governing Muslim divorce in Bangladesh. It makes notification of the Union Parishad Chairman mandatory and imposes the 90-day waiting period. Failure to comply is a criminal offence."
  - question: "How long does Muslim divorce take in Bangladesh?"
    answer: "Minimum 90 days from the date the UP Chairman receives the notice (for talaq, khula, mubarat). Judicial divorce in Family Court can take 6 months to several years."
  - question: "What is talaq-e-tawfiz and how does it work in Bangladesh?"
    answer: "Talaq-e-tawfiz is a delegated right of divorce granted to the wife in the Kabinnama. If included, the wife can pronounce divorce herself under specified conditions without needing to file in court."
